üìú MPC ¬ß 1.02(2) ‚Äî The Evaluative Standard

Purposes; Principles of Construction

The general purposes of the provisions governing the sentencing and treatment of offenders are:

  • to prevent the commission of offenses;
  • to promote the correction and rehabilitation of offenders;
  • to safeguard offenders against excessive, disproportionate or arbitrary punishment;
  • to give fair warning of the nature of the sentences that may be imposed;
  • to differentiate among offenders with a view to a just individualization;
  • to harmonize the powers, duties and functions of courts and agencies;
  • to advance the use of scientific methods in sentencing;
  • to integrate responsibility for the correctional system.

The Core Tension

"[I]ndividualized justice is prima facie at war with such concepts, at least as fundamental, as equality, objectivity, and consistency in the law."

‚Äî Judge Marvin Frankel (via 16__Anderson 1999, p. 276)

How We Reframe the MPC

MPC Principle Anti-MM Reading Pro-MM Reading
(c) Safeguard against arbitrary Judges need discretion Discretion IS the source of arbitrariness
(d) Fair warning MMs are too rigid MMs provide the clearest warning possible
(e) Individualization MMs prevent it Pre-reform "individualization" = judge's bias
(f) Harmonize courts MMs override judges MMs harmonize by removing judge variance
üéØ Our Thesis

The MPC's goals of equality, objectivity, and consistency require constraints on discretion. Mandatory minimums are one such constraint.

üèõÔ∏è The Three Branches & Sentencing Power

Understanding which branch should control sentencing is fundamental to the MM debate. Each branch has a different design, purpose, and relationship to democratic accountability.

Core Functions by Design

üèõÔ∏è

LEGISLATIVE

Purpose: MAKE the laws

  • Elected by the people (House every 2 yrs, Senate every 6)
  • Represents the will and moral values of the people
  • Public debate, recorded votes, transparent process
  • Deliberative ‚Äî slow by design to build consensus

In sentencing: Sets the rules ‚Äî what's a crime, the range of punishment, minimums and maximums

üè¢

EXECUTIVE

Purpose: ENFORCE the laws

  • President elected every 4 years
  • Prosecutors appointed, serve at pleasure of AG
  • Implements what the legislature decides
  • Discretion in enforcement (can't prosecute everyone)

In sentencing: Decides who to charge and what to charge them with; negotiates pleas

‚öñÔ∏è

JUDICIAL

Purpose: INTERPRET the laws

  • Federal judges: life tenure, appointed not elected
  • Designed to be insulated from politics
  • Independent, neutral arbiter
  • Applies law to specific cases, not popular opinion

In sentencing: Applies the sentence within the range set by legislature to individual cases

The Separation of Powers Design

The Founders designed a system of checks and balances ‚Äî no single branch should have too much power:

Branch Makes Law? Enforces Law? Interprets Law?
Legislative ‚úÖ Yes ‚ùå No ‚ùå No
Executive ‚ùå No ‚úÖ Yes ‚ùå No
Judicial ‚ùå No ‚ùå No ‚úÖ Yes

When one branch starts doing another branch's job, the constitutional design breaks down.

üß≠ Which Branch Reflects the People's Moral Values?

Sentencing is fundamentally a moral question: How seriously should society treat this crime? What punishment is just? These are value judgments that should reflect the people's evolving moral consensus.

Branch Reflects Changing Morals? How / Why Not?
üèõÔ∏è Legislative ‚úÖ YES ‚Äî by design
  • Elections force responsiveness to changing values
  • Public debate surfaces moral shifts
  • Slow deliberation builds durable consensus
  • Amendments can update laws as morals evolve
  • Examples: Fair Sentencing Act 2010, First Step Act 2018
üè¢ Executive ‚ö†Ô∏è Somewhat ‚Äî but crudely
  • Policy swings wildly every 4-8 years
  • One person (AG) decides, not 535 deliberating
  • No public debate ‚Äî policy-by-memo
  • Whiplash: Holder lenient ‚Üí Sessions harsh ‚Üí Garland lenient
  • Not moral deliberation ‚Äî political/strategic shifts
‚öñÔ∏è Judicial ‚ùå NO ‚Äî by design
  • Life tenure ‚Äî no mechanism to update
  • Judge appointed in 1995 still sentencing with 1995 values
  • Explicitly counter-majoritarian ‚Äî insulated from popular opinion
  • No elections, no recall (federal), no accountability
  • Example: Judge Persky's 1980s values in 2016 ‚Üí Brock Turner

üí° The Insight

The legislative branch is the only branch structurally designed to reflect and update the people's moral values over time. That's why sentencing policy ‚Äî the moral judgment of how seriously to treat crimes ‚Äî belongs with Congress, not with unelected judges or unaccountable prosecutors.

üéöÔ∏è The Legislative Branch's Sentencing Tools

Congress has only two levers to express the people's moral judgment about punishment:

The CEILING
How high can punishment go?

üìâ

That's it. Those are the only tools Congress has to express the people's moral judgment about punishment severity.

üîÑ Who Fills the Power Vacuum?

When you remove the legislative branch's floor, that power doesn't disappear ‚Äî it shifts:

‚öñÔ∏è Judges Gain Power

  • Unelected, life tenure
  • Answer to no one
  • Apply their own moral values case-by-case
  • No mechanism to update when out of step with society
  • This is "legislating from the bench"

üè¢ Prosecutors Gain Power

  • Operate behind closed doors
  • Almost no judicial review (Wayte)
  • Make strategic decisions, not moral ones
  • No public deliberation
  • Policy-by-memo, not democratic process

‚ö†Ô∏è Neither of These Actors Represents the People

Neither judges nor prosecutors face voters. Neither has to publicly defend their sentencing decisions. Neither engages in democratic deliberation about what punishments are just. When you abolish MMs, you transfer the people's moral voice from their elected representatives to unaccountable actors.

‚öñÔ∏è The Constitutional Problem

The Founders designed a system where:

  • Congress makes the law (including sentencing policy)
  • Judges apply the law to individual cases
  • Executive enforces the law

Abolishing MMs inverts this design:

  • Judges make sentencing policy case-by-case (legislative function)
  • Congress is reduced to setting meaningless ceilings
  • The people's voice is removed from the process entirely

üìú The System Working: Congress Updating Moral Judgments

When society's moral views shift, Congress can update sentencing law through democratic deliberation:

Moral Shift Legislative Response
Crack/powder disparity recognized as racially unjust Fair Sentencing Act 2010 ‚Äî reduced ratio from 100:1 to 18:1
Recognition that low-level offenders deserve relief First Step Act 2018 ‚Äî expanded safety valve, made changes retroactive
Evolving views on marijuana Multiple decriminalization bills advancing through Congress

This is the system working. The legislature updated moral judgments through democratic deliberation ‚Äî not one judge's opinion.

‚ùì What's the Judicial Alternative?

If you abolish MMs and give judges total discretion, how do you update sentencing when society's values change?

  • Wait for old judges to die and hope their replacements have different values?
  • Hope for a different random assignment in your case?
  • Accept that a Reagan-appointee will sentence with 1985 moral views in 2025?

That's not a system for updating moral consensus ‚Äî that's a lottery.

üé§ Debate Lines

The Core Constitutional Argument

"You want to abolish mandatory minimums. But MMs are the only tool Congress has to set a floor ‚Äî to say 'this crime is at least THIS serious.' Take that away, and what's left? Advisory guidelines that judges can ignore. Sky-high ceilings that never bind. You're not 'restoring judicial discretion' ‚Äî you're removing the legislative branch from sentencing entirely. You're cutting the democratic voice out of criminal justice and handing it to unelected judges with life tenure. Is that really what you want?"

On Moral Judgments

"What is the appropriate punishment for selling 500 grams of cocaine? That's a moral question. It reflects society's values about harm, accountability, and justice. Who should answer that question ‚Äî 535 elected representatives who debated it publicly and face voters, or one unelected judge with life tenure who answers to no one? MMs are Congress saying: 'We, the people's representatives, have made a moral judgment about the minimum seriousness of this crime.' Abolishing them transfers that moral judgment to individual judges ‚Äî each with their own idiosyncratic values and biases ‚Äî with zero democratic input."

On Updating Values

"Moral views change. In 1986, crack cocaine was seen as an existential threat; Congress set harsh MMs. By 2010, society recognized the racial inequity; Congress passed the Fair Sentencing Act and reduced them. That's the system working ‚Äî the legislature updated the moral judgment through democratic deliberation. What's your alternative? Wait for Reagan-appointed judges to die and hope their replacements have different values? That's not accountability ‚Äî that's a generational lottery."

On Judicial "Discretion"

"You call it 'judicial discretion.' I call it judicial policymaking. When a judge decides 'I think drug crimes should get lighter sentences,' that's not interpreting law ‚Äî that's making policy. And policy should be made by elected legislators who face voters, not by unelected judges with life tenure who face no one. That's not discretion ‚Äî that's judicial supremacy over democratic values."

üí° Why This Matters for the MM Debate

Mandatory minimums are the only legislative tool that sets a binding floor. When you remove them:

  • The legislative branch loses its only mechanism to ensure minimum accountability
  • Power shifts to prosecutors (charging) and judges (discretion)
  • Neither prosecutors nor judges are as democratically accountable as Congress
  • The other tools still exist ‚Äî they just operate without a legislative floor

The debate over MMs is really a debate over whether the legislative branch should have a binding voice in sentencing.

Pre-1984: Indeterminate

Judges had near-total discretion. Parole boards adjusted actual time served. Same crime ‚Üí wildly different sentences. Frankel called it "law without order."

1984‚Äì2005: Mandatory Guidelines

Congress took control. Guidelines were binding. MMs proliferated. Parole abolished. Judges became "sentencing calculators." Power shifted to prosecutors.

Guidelines advisory. Judicial discretion restored. But MMs still binding ‚Üí prosecutors still control exposure. Result: racial disparity increased 75% (Yang).

üí° The Key Insight

Power doesn't disappear ‚Äî it shifts. When you remove or weaken one lever, another lever absorbs that power. Abolishing MMs doesn't eliminate discretion; it transfers power from the legislative branch (elected, public) to the judicial branch (unelected, case-by-case) and executive branch (hidden plea deals).

üí≥ The "Dave Ramsey Fallacy"

Dave Ramsey on Credit Cards:

  • Credit cards are bad
  • They cause debt and harm
  • Solution: eliminate them entirely
  • No nuance about responsible use

Anti-MM Side on Statutory Minimums:

  • MMs are bad
  • They cause disparity and harsh sentences
  • Solution: abolish them entirely
  • No nuance about calibration

The Reality:

Debt isn't inherently good or bad ‚Äî it's a tool. A mortgage builds wealth. A business loan creates jobs. But misused debt destroys lives. The question isn't whether to use debt ‚Äî it's how to use it responsibly.

Nobody serious says "abolish all debt." We talk about appropriate limits, responsible use, when it makes sense. The same logic applies to statutory minimums ‚Äî they're a lever, not a moral failing.

üéØ Debate Line

"The other side takes the Dave Ramsey approach to sentencing ‚Äî just label MMs as 'bad' and eliminate them entirely. But that's not how complex systems work. Debt isn't inherently bad; it's a tool. MMs aren't inherently bad; they're a lever. The question isn't whether to have them ‚Äî it's how to calibrate them responsibly."

The Calibration Argument

The sophisticated position isn't "MMs good" or "MMs bad." It's: How do we calibrate all the levers to best achieve the MPC's competing goals?

Reasonable reforms might include:

  • Expanding safety valves (more exceptions for low-level offenders)
  • Lowering some floors (recalibrating, not abolishing)
  • Constraining prosecutorial discretion (charging guidelines, transparency)
  • Strengthening appellate review (check on judicial discretion)

But abolition isn't calibration. It's removing the legislative branch's only meaningful lever on the floor ‚Äî and hoping the judicial and executive branches produce better outcomes. Booker showed us what happens when you try that.

üéØ The Real Problem: Unchecked Prosecutorial Power

Here's the insight that reframes the entire debate:

The problem with MMs isn't MMs themselves ‚Äî it's that we've constrained judicial discretion without equally constraining prosecutorial discretion. We created a system where prosecutors have more power than ever, with almost no accountability.

‚öñÔ∏è Judges

  • Decisions on the record
  • Must state reasons
  • Subject to appellate review
  • Constrained by Guidelines + MMs

üè¢ Prosecutors

  • Decisions behind closed doors
  • No requirement to explain
  • Almost no review (Wayte doctrine ‚Äî see below)
  • Constrained by... DOJ memos?

The Better Reform Agenda

Instead of abolishing MMs (which just shifts disparity to judges), we should ask: How do we constrain prosecutorial discretion?

  • Charging guidelines ‚Äî binding rules on when to charge MM-triggering offenses
  • Transparency requirements ‚Äî document and report charging decisions by race, district
  • Judicial review of charging ‚Äî allow defendants to challenge disparate charging
  • Limit plea bargaining leverage ‚Äî reduce the "trial penalty"
  • Open-file discovery ‚Äî reduce information asymmetry
üéØ Reframe the Debate

"The other side wants to abolish MMs to fix disparity. But that just moves disparity from prosecutors to judges ‚Äî and judicial disparity is actually more visible and more reviewable. If you're serious about disparity, the real question is: why haven't we constrained prosecutorial discretion the way we've constrained judges? That's where the hidden disparity lives. That's the reform we actually need."

‚öñÔ∏è The Hidden Enabler: Wayte v. United States (1985)

Everyone talks about MMs giving prosecutors power. But there's a case lurking in the background that no one talks about ‚Äî and it's the real reason prosecutorial discretion is almost unchallengeable:

Wayte v. United States, 470 U.S. 598 (1985)

To challenge a prosecutor's charging decision as "selective prosecution," a defendant must prove both:

  1. Discriminatory effect ‚Äî the policy disproportionately affected a protected group
  2. Discriminatory purpose ‚Äî the prosecutor intended to discriminate
Why This Matters

Challenging a Judge

Show the sentence was "unreasonable" ‚Üí Appellate court reviews the record ‚Üí Relief possible

Challenging a Prosecutor

Prove discriminatory intent ‚Üí But decisions are behind closed doors ‚Üí No record exists ‚Üí Nearly impossible

Even if you can show Black defendants are charged with MM-triggering offenses at 2x the rate of white defendants for similar conduct (discriminatory effect), you still lose under Wayte unless you can prove the prosecutor intended to discriminate.

Since charging decisions happen behind closed doors with no documentation requirement, proving intent is virtually impossible. Wayte effectively immunizes prosecutors from accountability.

üí° The Insight

MMs don't give prosecutors unchecked power ‚Äî Wayte does. MMs just give prosecutors more tools to use with that already-unchecked power. If you want to fix prosecutorial disparity, you don't abolish MMs ‚Äî you overturn or limit Wayte, or create statutory transparency requirements that don't depend on proving intent.

üéØ Debate Line

"The other side blames MMs for prosecutorial disparity. But MMs aren't the source of prosecutorial power ‚Äî Wayte v. United States is. That 1985 case made it nearly impossible to challenge a prosecutor's charging decision, even with statistical proof of racial disparity. Abolishing MMs doesn't touch Wayte. Prosecutors will still have unchecked discretion ‚Äî they'll just exercise it through different tools. If you're serious about prosecutorial accountability, talk about Wayte. üé≠ The Nirvana Fallacy: The Anti-MM Argument's Fatal Flaw

The anti-MM side's entire argumentative structure relies on a logical fallacy. Once you see it, you can call it out every time they make a point.

What Is the Nirvana Fallacy?

Comparing a real option (with all its flaws) to an idealized alternative (where the flaws magically disappear) ‚Äî rather than to the actual alternative (which has its own flaws).

Also known as the "perfect solution fallacy" or "grass-is-greener fallacy."

How the Anti-MM Argument Works

Step 1

"MMs cause [bad thing]"

disparity, disproportionality, prosecutorial power, etc.

‚Üí

Step 2

"Therefore, abolish MMs"

Implicit: The bad thing disappears

‚ö†Ô∏è The Hidden Assumption

They're comparing:

Real MMs (with all their flaws) vs. Idealized No-MMs (where flaws magically disappear)

They're NOT comparing:

Real MMs (with flaws) vs. Real No-MMs (with different flaws)

Every Anti-MM Argument Uses This Fallacy

They Say The Nirvana Fallacy The Reality
"MMs cause racial disparity" Abolishing MMs = no disparity Disparity shifts to judges
(Yang: +75% post-Booker)
"MMs give prosecutors too much power" Abolishing MMs = balanced power Power shifts to judges
(life tenure, no accountability)
"MMs are disproportionate" Abolishing MMs = proportionality Judges give wildly different sentences
(Second Circuit: 3 yrs vs 20 yrs same crime)
"MMs remove judicial discretion" Abolishing MMs = good discretion Unconstrained discretion = Brock Turner
(6 months for rape)
"MMs don't deter crime" Abolishing MMs = better deterrence No evidence judicial discretion deters either
(Nagin: certainty matters, not source)
"MMs shift power from judges to prosecutors" Abolishing MMs = power back to judges Federal judges have life tenure, no recall
(Less accountable than prosecutors)
"MMs create cliff effects (499g vs 501g)" Abolishing MMs = no arbitrary lines All legal thresholds have cliff effects
(BAC 0.079 vs 0.08, age 17 vs 18)

üí° The Core Insight

The problems they cite don't disappear when you abolish MMs ‚Äî they shift to different actors.

  • Racial disparity doesn't disappear ‚Äî it shifts from prosecutors to judges
  • Power doesn't disappear ‚Äî it shifts from the legislative branch to the judicial branch
  • Disproportionality doesn't disappear ‚Äî it just becomes inconsistent in a different way
  • Arbitrariness doesn't disappear ‚Äî it shifts from statutory thresholds to judicial whim

üéØ How to Call It Out

The Basic Call-Out

"That's the nirvana fallacy. You're telling me what's wrong with MMs, but you're not telling me why the alternative is better. Show me the world without MMs is actually better ‚Äî not just different."

On Racial Disparity

"You say MMs cause racial disparity. The Yang study says judicial discretion causes more racial disparity ‚Äî 75% more after Booker. So you're not eliminating disparity; you're moving it from prosecutors to judges. If disparity exists either way, 'disparity' isn't a reason to choose one over the other. It's a wash. You need a different argument."

On Prosecutorial Power

"You say MMs give prosecutors too much power. But abolishing MMs doesn't eliminate that power ‚Äî it transfers it to federal judges with life tenure who can't be voted out, recalled, or fired. You're trading elected accountability for zero accountability. How is that better?"

On Any Anti-MM Argument

"You've identified a flaw in the current system. But you haven't shown me the alternative is better. What does 'no MMs' actually look like? Not in theory ‚Äî in practice. Show me the data that judicial discretion produces better outcomes than MMs. Because right now, you're comparing a real system to an imaginary one."

The Potholes Analogy

"Using racial disparity to argue against MMs is like saying 'this road has potholes, so let's take the other road' ‚Äî without checking if the other road has even more potholes. The Yang study checked. It does."

üîÑ The Reframe

Once you've exposed the nirvana fallacy, reframe the debate:

"The question isn't 'are MMs perfect?' ‚Äî nothing is. The question is: 'Do MMs produce better outcomes than the alternative?' And the alternative isn't some idealized system of wise judges making perfect decisions. The alternative is the pre-1984 system where sentences ranged from 3 to 20 years for the same crime, where racial disparity was rampant, and where the legislative branch had no voice in sentencing. That's what you're advocating for. Is that actually better?"

  • "You've told me what's wrong with MMs. Now tell me: what's the actual alternative, and why is it better?"
  • "If we abolish MMs, where does the disparity go? Does it disappear, or does it just shift?"
  • "Show me the data that judicial discretion produces less racial disparity than MMs."
  • "You're comparing real MMs to imaginary no-MMs. Compare real MMs to real no-MMs ‚Äî the pre-1984 system. Was that better?"
